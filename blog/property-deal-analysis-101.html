Property Deal Analysis 101

Learn how professional investors evaluate property deals — the metrics, the benchmarks, and the one number that reveals the true picture.

By Ateeq Yousif · May 14, 2026 · 9 min read

A property that looks good on the surface can be a terrible investment once you run the numbers. Professional investors know this. They do not fall in love with properties — they fall in love with numbers. This guide teaches you the essential metrics every UK property investor needs to evaluate a deal properly, along with real benchmarks so you know what "good" looks like.

Gross Rental Yield

Formula: (Annual Rental Income ÷ Property Purchase Price) × 100

Gross yield is the starting point of any deal analysis. It tells you at a glance how much income the property generates relative to its cost. A property with a 7% gross yield in Liverpool is obviously more income-efficient than one with a 3% gross yield in London. However, gross yield ignores all costs — mortgage, management, maintenance, insurance, voids — so it can be misleading on its own.

Benchmark: 6%+ is good for single lets in the North. 4-5% is reasonable for Midlands and Southern cities. Below 4% needs exceptional capital growth prospects to be worthwhile.

Net Rental Yield

Formula: ((Annual Rental Income − Annual Costs) ÷ Property Purchase Price) × 100

Net yield is gross yield minus all the real-world costs of owning and managing the property. These include mortgage interest, letting agent fees (8-15% of rent), buildings insurance, maintenance reserve, and void periods. This is the number that tells you the actual cash return on the property before tax.

Benchmark: 4%+ net yield is solid. 5%+ net is very good. 2-3% net means you are relying entirely on capital appreciation for your returns.

Monthly Cash Flow

Formula: Monthly Rental Income − Monthly Mortgage Payment − Monthly Costs

This is the most intuitive metric for most investors. How much money lands in your bank account each month after all bills are paid? A positive monthly cash flow means the property pays for itself. Negative cash flow means you are subsidising the investment from your salary or other income.

Benchmark: £200-£400 per month is a solid single-let cash flow for a terraced house in the North. £100-£200 is acceptable if capital growth prospects are strong. Below £100 puts most investors in the "is it worth it?" zone.

Cash-on-Cash ROI

Formula: (Annual Pre-Tax Cash Flow ÷ Total Cash Invested) × 100

This is the metric that tells you how efficiently your capital is working. If you invested £50,000 as a deposit and costs, and the property generates £4,000 per year in cash flow, your cash-on-cash ROI is 8%. This is directly comparable to other investments — shares, bonds, or a second property.

Benchmark: 6-10% cash-on-cash ROI is the target range for standard single-let BTL. BRRR deals often produce 30-100% ROI on the minimal capital retained after refinance because the metric denominator is so small.

Total Return: The Full Picture

Many new investors focus only on cash flow and miss the bigger picture. Total return includes both income (cash flow) and capital appreciation. Over a 10-year hold period, a property that generates modest cash flow but appreciates 4% per year will likely outperform one with strong cash flow but zero appreciation.

Formula: Total Return = (Cumulative Cash Flow + Capital Growth − Sale Costs) ÷ Total Investment × 100

However, total return is a backward-looking metric. You can project it, but the actual result depends on future market conditions. This is why most experienced investors focus on cash flow (which they can control and predict) and treat capital appreciation as a bonus.

Deal Analysis Benchmarks

Here are the benchmarks professional UK investors use:

Yield: Gross yield ≥ 6%, Net yield ≥ 4% for northern cities. Adjust expectations down 1-2% for London and the South East.

Cash flow: £200+ positive cash flow per month per property after all costs. Portfolio landlords target £1,000+ per month total across their portfolio.

Cash-on-cash ROI: ≥ 8% for a standard buy-to-let. ≥ 15% for a deal requiring active management (HMO, serviced accommodation, BRRR).

Interest coverage ratio: ≥ 125% at the lender's stress rate. Your own target should be ≥ 130% to give a buffer against rate rises or void periods.

Capital growth: 3-5% annualised over the hold period. This varies enormously by location. Northern cities are currently delivering 4-7%, while London is closer to 1-3%.

The One Metric That Matters Most

If you can only track one number, track net yield. It incorporates all costs, all income, and gives you the true cash return on the property. A property with good net yield will almost always have positive cash flow and reasonable ROI. A property with poor net yield needs exceptional capital growth to justify itself — and capital growth is the one thing you cannot guarantee.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is a good yield for a first buy-to-let? For a first BTL with a 25% deposit, aim for a net yield of 4% or above. This gives you a comfortable margin above mortgage costs and leaves room for voids and repairs. In practice, this means targeting gross yields of 6%+ in most markets.

Should I accept negative cash flow for capital growth? Generally no for a leveraged investor. Negative cash flow means you are paying to hold the property. If interest rates rise or the market turns, you could be forced to sell at a bad time. Cash flow positive properties let you wait out market cycles.

How do I account for inflation in deal analysis? The simplest approach is to assume rents grow at 2-3% per year and costs grow at a similar rate. This keeps the real (inflation-adjusted) return roughly constant over the hold period. More sophisticated investors build in specific inflation scenarios.

What costs do new investors typically forget? The most commonly forgotten costs are: letting agent setup fees (often one month's rent + VAT), EPC certification (£50-£120), gas safety certificate (£60-£100), landlord insurance (£150-£300/year), and void periods (budget 4 weeks per year).

Is a higher ROI always better? Not necessarily. A 20% ROI on a property where you have invested £10,000 is £2,000 per year. An 8% ROI on a £100,000 investment is £8,000 per year. Total return in pounds matters more than percentage return. Use the property deal analyser to compare both metrics.
