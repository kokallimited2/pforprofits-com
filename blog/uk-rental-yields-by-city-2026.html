UK Rental Yields by City 2026

Where should you invest for the best cash flow? We analyse gross and net rental yields across 15 UK cities for buy-to-let investors.

By Ateeq Yousif · May 14, 2026 · 10 min read

Rental yield is the single most important metric for a cash-flow-focused property investor. It tells you how much income your property generates relative to its purchase price. In this guide, we compare average rental yields across 15 UK cities in 2026, break down gross versus net yields, and identify the best places to invest for monthly cash flow.

Rental Yield Overview 2026

UK rental yields have shifted noticeably in 2026. Rising interest rates have increased mortgage costs, compressing net yields across the board. However, rental demand remains strong — average rents have risen 5-7% year-on-year in most cities — which has partly offset higher financing costs.

The result is a market where location matters more than ever. The gap between the best and worst performing cities has widened. Northern cities continue to offer the strongest cash flow, while yields in London and the South East remain under pressure from high property prices relative to rents.

Rental Yields by City: Full Comparison

These figures are based on average property prices and rents across each city for a typical 2-3 bedroom terraced house. Gross yield is annual rent divided by purchase price. Net yield includes mortgage interest (at 4.5% for a 75% LTV mortgage), letting agent fees at 10%, insurance, and maintenance reserve at 1% of property value.

Top Yields: 6-8% Gross

Liverpool — Gross: 7.2% | Net: 4.8%. Liverpool remains the UK's yield king. Average house prices around £160,000 with rents averaging £960 per month. Strong student and young professional demand keeps void periods low.

Glasgow — Gross: 7.0% | Net: 4.6%. Scotland's largest city offers excellent value. Average prices around £150,000, average rent £875 per month. The Scottish rental market has strong tenant protections but remains attractive for cash flow investors.

Sunderland — Gross: 6.8% | Net: 4.4%. Low property prices (£120,000 average) generate strong yields despite lower absolute rents (£680/month). Good for investors with smaller budgets.

Nottingham — Gross: 6.5% | Net: 4.2%. A strong student and professional market. Average prices around £190,000, average rent £1,030 per month.

Solid Yields: 5-6% Gross

Manchester — Gross: 6.0% | Net: 3.8%. The UK's strongest capital growth story. Prices have risen steadily but rents have kept pace. Average price £220,000, average rent £1,100 per month.

Leeds — Gross: 5.8% | Net: 3.7%. Strong rental demand from students and young professionals. Average price £200,000, average rent £970 per month.

Birmingham — Gross: 5.5% | Net: 3.5%. The UK's second city offers decent yields with strong capital growth potential. Average price £230,000, average rent £1,050 per month.

Sheffield — Gross: 5.4% | Net: 3.3%. Affordable property prices (£185,000 average) with steady rental demand. A solid choice for conservative investors.

Lower Yields: 3-5% Gross

Bristol — Gross: 4.8% | Net: 2.8%. Strong capital growth but yields are squeezed by high prices (£340,000 average). Rent at £1,360 per month.

Edinburgh — Gross: 4.5% | Net: 2.6%. Premium property prices (£290,000 average) with good rental demand from professionals and students.

Reading — Gross: 4.2% | Net: 2.3%. London commuter belt. High prices (£370,000) but excellent employment market supports rents.

London (Greater) — Gross: 4.0% | Net: 2.0%. London-wide average. Zone-specific yields range from 3.0% in prime central to 5.0% in outer zones like Barking or Dagenham.

Gross vs Net Yield: What Actually Matters

Too many investors chase gross yield without running the net figures. A property with 8% gross yield but high management costs, expensive maintenance, and a large mortgage could leave you with less cash flow than a 5% gross yield property with lower financing costs.

Gross yield is: (Annual Rent ÷ Purchase Price) × 100. It is useful for comparing properties at a glance.

Net yield is: ((Annual Rent − Annual Costs) ÷ Purchase Price) × 100. Annual costs include mortgage interest (not capital repayment), letting agent fees (typically 8-15% of rent), buildings insurance (£150-300/year), maintenance reserve (1% of property value), and void periods (allow 4 weeks per year).

Yield Trends to Watch

Three trends are shaping UK rental yields in 2026:

1. The North-South yield gap is widening. Northern cities now offer 2-3 percentage points more gross yield than southern equivalents. This gap is likely to persist while southern property prices remain elevated relative to wages and rents.

2. Higher interest rates are compressing net yields. With BTL mortgage rates at 4-5%, the net yield on a typical 75% LTV property is 1.5-2 percentage points lower than gross. This makes all-cash purchases or lower-LTV deals increasingly attractive.

3. Rental growth is outpacing house price growth. In most UK cities, rents are growing at 5-7% annually while house prices are growing at 2-4%. This is slowly improving yields, particularly in cities with strong employment markets.

Bottom line: For cash flow, invest in the North — Liverpool, Glasgow, Manchester, and Nottingham offer the best combination of yield and capital growth potential. For capital appreciation with lower yield, look at London Zone 3-5 or South East cities with strong transport links.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is considered a good net rental yield? A good net yield is 4% or above after all costs. Anything above 6% net is excellent for a buy-to-let with standard financing. Cash buyers can achieve 7-9% net in top-yielding cities.

How does HMO yield compare? Houses in Multiple Occupation (HMOs) typically yield 10-15% gross, which is significantly higher than single-let properties. However, HMOs have higher management costs, stricter regulatory requirements, and higher tenant turnover.

Should I invest for yield or capital growth? It depends on your strategy. Cash flow investors prioritise yield and typically invest in northern cities. Capital growth investors accept lower yields for properties in areas with stronger price appreciation. Most successful investors combine both — using yield to cover costs while benefiting from long-term appreciation.

Are serviced accommodation yields higher? Short-term lets (Airbnb-style) can achieve 8-12% gross yield in tourist-heavy cities. However, regulatory changes in Scotland and proposed changes in England are making this strategy riskier. Check local rules before pursuing serviced accommodation.
