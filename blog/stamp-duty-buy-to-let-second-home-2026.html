Stamp Duty for Buy-to-Let and Second Homes 2026

Complete guide to stamp duty land tax (SDLT) for UK buy-to-let and additional property purchases — rates, surcharges, thresholds, and real examples.

By Ateeq Yousif · May 14, 2026 · 8 min read

Stamp duty land tax (SDLT) is one of the largest upfront costs when purchasing a buy-to-let or second home in England and Northern Ireland. For property investors, understanding how SDLT works — particularly the 3% surcharge — is essential for accurate deal analysis. A buy-to-let that looks profitable at the gross level can become marginal once the full SDLT bill is factored in.

Current SDLT Rates for Additional Properties (2026)

As of May 2026, the SDLT rates for buy-to-let and second homes in England and Northern Ireland are:

Up to £250,000: 0% standard + 3% surcharge = 3% total

£250,001 to £925,000: 5% standard + 3% surcharge = 8% total

£925,001 to £1.5 million: 10% standard + 3% surcharge = 13% total

Above £1.5 million: 12% standard + 3% surcharge = 15% total

The surcharge applies to the entire purchase price in each band, not just the amount above the first property threshold.

Real-World Examples

Example 1: £150,000 buy-to-let in Liverpool

Purchase price: £150,000
SDLT calculation: 3% of £150,000 = £4,500
Total SDLT: £4,500

At this price point, the stamp duty adds £4,500 to the purchase costs — roughly equivalent to 3 months of rental income on a standard terraced house in Liverpool.

Example 2: £250,000 buy-to-let in Manchester

Purchase price: £250,000
SDLT calculation: 3% of £250,000 = £7,500
Total SDLT: £7,500

This is a typical purchase price for a 3-bed house in a good Manchester suburb. The £7,500 stamp duty represents about 6 months of net rental income.

Example 3: £400,000 buy-to-let in London (Zone 4)

Purchase price: £400,000
SDLT calculation: 3% on first £250,000 = £7,500 + 8% on remaining £150,000 = £12,000
Total SDLT: £19,500

At this price point, stamp duty is a significant cost — nearly 5% of the purchase price. This is why accurate deal analysis is critical for London BTL purchases.

The 3% Surcharge Explained

The 3% surcharge on additional properties was introduced in April 2016 and remains in place. It applies to anyone buying a residential property in England or Northern Ireland who already owns a residential property anywhere in the world, unless:

  • You are replacing your main residence (selling your old home and buying a new one).
  • The property you own is worth less than £40,000.
  • The existing property is a caravan, houseboat, or mobile home.
  • You are a corporate buyer purchasing six or more properties in a single transaction (commercial SDLT rules apply instead).

The surcharge applies per-transaction, not per-property. So if you are a portfolio landlord who already owns five properties, you pay the surcharge on every new purchase.

Exceptions and Refunds

There are two important ways to reduce or reclaim SDLT on a buy-to-let purchase:

1. Selling your main residence within 36 months. If you buy a new main residence before selling your old one, you pay the 3% surcharge upfront. You can then reclaim it if you sell your old home within 36 months. The refund application must be made within 12 months of the sale completing.

2. First-time buyers. First-time buyers purchasing their first home do not pay SDLT on the first £425,000 (up to a £625,000 limit). However, this relief only applies if you do not already own a property anywhere in the world. It does not apply to buy-to-let purchases.

The refund process is handled through HMRC's SDLT repayment system. You will need to complete an SDLT return amendment and provide evidence of the sale of your previous main residence.

Scotland and Wales: Different Rules

If you are investing outside England or Northern Ireland, different rules apply.

Scotland (LBTT): Land and Buildings Transaction Tax (LBTT) replaced SDLT in Scotland in 2015. For additional properties, an extra 6% surcharge on bands up to £325,000 is payable. Above that, the surcharge structure differs. Scottish rates are generally more favourable at lower price points but the 6% surcharge is steeper than England's 3%.

Wales (LTT): Land Transaction Tax (LTT) replaced SDLT in Wales in 2018. An extra 4% surcharge applies to additional properties. The Welsh rates and bands differ from England's, so always check the latest Welsh Revenue Authority guidance.

How SDLT Affects Your Deal Analysis

Stamp duty is typically one of your largest upfront costs after the deposit. It directly affects your total investment and therefore your cash-on-cash ROI. When running deal analysis, always include SDLT in the total cost calculation.

For a typical BRRR deal, SDLT is part of the "purchase costs" that increase your total investment. This makes the refinance target harder to hit because you need to pull more capital back. For a standard buy-to-let, higher SDLT means lower net yield and a longer breakeven period.

Quick tip: For buy-to-let deals under £250,000, the SDLT bill is exactly 3% of the purchase price. You do not need a calculator for these — just multiply the purchase price by 0.03. For higher-priced properties, use HMRC's SDLT calculator or factor the banded rates into your overall deal analysis.

Frequently Asked Questions

Does the 3% surcharge apply to limited company purchases? Yes. If a company buys a residential property, the 3% surcharge applies unless the company owns six or more properties in a single transaction. This is a common trap for new limited company investors.

Can I avoid SDLT by buying through a company? No. The 3% surcharge for additional properties also applies to most corporate purchases. There is no straightforward way to avoid SDLT on buy-to-let purchases. Attempting to do so through artificial structures can trigger HMRC anti-avoidance rules.

Is SDLT calculated on the purchase price or the valuation? SDLT is calculated on the purchase price, not the market valuation. However, if the purchase price is significantly below market value (as in a below-market-value BRRR purchase), HMRC may apply SDLT on the market value instead. This is known as the "connected party" or "transfer at undervalue" rule.

How does stamp duty affect my rental yield calculation? SDLT increases your total investment, which directly reduces your net yield. For example, on a £200,000 property with £1,000/month rent, adding £7,500 SDLT increases your effective cost base to £207,500, dropping your gross yield from 6.0% to 5.8% and your net yield by a similar proportion.

Do I pay SDLT on the purchase of a property portfolio? If you purchase six or more residential properties in a single transaction, you can elect for non-residential SDLT rates, which have different bands and rates. This can result in lower SDLT for very large portfolio purchases. Consult a specialist property solicitor for multi-property purchases.
